How a Stubborn Green Film in a Dirty Beaker Learned to Guard Our Food

In 1933, as the Great Depression gripped the world and Franklin Roosevelt promised Americans a New Deal, a young Dow Chemical lab worker named Ralph Wiley was facing a far humbler problem: he could not get his glassware clean. Prohibition had just ended, King Kong was climbing the Empire State Building on cinema screens, and in a Michigan laboratory a stubborn, dark film clung to the inside of a vial and simply refused to be scrubbed away. Most workers would have cursed the mess and moved on. Instead, that unscrubbable residue turned out to be an entirely new polymer — one that would go on to keep the world's leftovers fresh.

The film Wiley couldn't remove was polyvinylidene chloride, and its most striking quality was precisely the one that had frustrated him: it was almost impossibly impervious. Water, air, grease, and aroma all found it nearly impossible to pass through. What began as a cleaning nuisance was, in truth, one of the finest barrier materials ever stumbled upon — a molecular wall that Dow would christen with the now-famous name Saran.

### Molecular Architecture: A Wall Built of Chlorine

The secret to PVDC's remarkable barrier lies in its dense, tightly packed structure. Each repeating unit carries two chlorine atoms, large and heavy, that pull the chains into a highly crystalline, closely knit arrangement. Picture a brick wall in which every brick is oversized and perfectly fitted — there is simply no room for wandering molecules of oxygen or water vapor to slip through. This is why PVDC boasts one of the lowest permeabilities of any common plastic, a property that translates directly into longer-lasting food.

With a density around 1.7 g/cm³ — noticeably heavier than most everyday plastics, thanks to all that chlorine — PVDC is also self-extinguishing, resistant to acids and solvents, and stubbornly stable. It is not the material's strength or clarity that makes it precious, but its sheer refusal to let anything pass. In the world of packaging, being an excellent gatekeeper is worth more than being beautiful.

### Manufacturing Journey: From Military Coating to Kitchen Drawer

PVDC's early life was not in the kitchen at all. During the Second World War, Dow's green film found its first serious calling as a protective coating, sprayed onto fighter planes to shield them from corrosive sea spray and used to wrap military equipment against the elements. Its imperviousness, once a laboratory annoyance, became a wartime asset.

After the war, Dow set about taming the material for civilian life — stripping away its unpleasant green color and odor to produce a clear, clean film. In 1949, Saran Wrap arrived in American kitchens, and the polymer that had once resisted a dishwasher's scrubbing now clung faithfully to bowls and plates. Today PVDC is more often used as a thin barrier layer sandwiched inside multilayer films and coatings than as a standalone wrap, quietly lending its impermeability to packaging that must keep oxygen and moisture at bay.

### Applications and Impact: The Guardian of Freshness

For decades, PVDC has been the invisible guardian standing between food and spoilage. Its extraordinary barrier to oxygen slows the staling and rancidity that ruin perishable goods, while its resistance to moisture and aroma keeps flavors locked in and odors locked out. Cheeses, cured meats, and countless packaged foods owe their shelf life in part to a whisper-thin layer of this chlorine-rich polymer.

Beyond the kitchen, PVDC coats pharmaceutical blister packs to protect sensitive medicines from humidity, lines industrial pipes against corrosion, and appears in fibers, screens, and specialty films. Its trade name Saran became so familiar that it slid into everyday speech as a generic word for cling film — a rare linguistic honor, shared with the likes of Kleenex, that marks a material's deep entanglement with ordinary life.

### A Barrier Reconsidered

PVDC's very strength — its high chlorine content — has become the subject of modern environmental scrutiny, and some manufacturers have shifted toward chlorine-free alternatives for certain uses. Yet for applications where barrier performance is non-negotiable, few materials match its ability to hold back the invasion of air and moisture, and researchers continue to refine thinner, more efficient PVDC layers that deliver the same protection with less material.

From an unscrubbable stain in a Depression-era beaker to a global mainstay of food preservation, PVDC is a testament to the value of paying attention to accidents. Ralph Wiley's stubborn green film reminds us that the properties which frustrate us in one context can, in another, become exactly what the world needs.
