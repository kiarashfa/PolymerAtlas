From an Insoluble Powder Nobody Wanted to the Backbone of Carbon Fiber

In 1930, as the Great Depression settled over the world and Pluto was newly discovered at the edge of the solar system, two German chemists named Hans Fikentscher and Claus Heuck created a new polymer in the laboratories of IG Farben — and promptly ran into a wall. Their creation, polyacrylonitrile, was a fine white powder that stubbornly refused to melt and dissolved in none of the solvents available at the time. It was, by the standards of the day, useless. Research on it was quietly shelved, and the material seemed destined to be a footnote.

But this awkward, intractable powder was hiding an extraordinary future. The very qualities that made it so hard to work with — its resistance to heat and its tightly bound molecular structure — were the seeds of its destiny. Once chemists learned how to dissolve and spin it, polyacrylonitrile would become the precursor to nearly all the world's high-quality carbon fiber, the material skeleton of modern aircraft, rockets, and racing machines.

### Molecular Architecture: The Stubborn Chain

Polyacrylonitrile is built on a simple polyethylene-like backbone, but hanging from every other carbon is a nitrile group (–C≡N), a small, intensely polar appendage that changes everything. These nitrile groups bristle along the chain, and their strong dipoles pull neighboring chains together with such force that the polymer becomes rigid, semicrystalline, and fiercely resistant to solvents. It is this powerful intermolecular attraction that gives PAN its strength — and that made it so maddeningly insoluble to its discoverers.

PAN does not melt in the ordinary sense; heated too quickly, it degrades before it flows, and above about 180°C the nitrile groups begin to cyclize, releasing energy as they lock into ladder-like ring structures. Far from a defect, this cyclization is the key to PAN's greatest role: it is the first step on the path to turning a polymer fiber into pure carbon.

### Manufacturing Journey: Learning to Tame the Untameable

The breakthrough that rescued PAN from obscurity came from solving its solubility problem. In 1931, the chemist Herbert Rein, visiting the Ludwigshafen works, obtained a sample and discovered that certain unusual solvents could dissolve it; by 1938 he had spun the first PAN-based fibers, and in 1942 he identified dimethylformamide as an even better solvent, opening the door to practical production. Modern PAN is almost always made as a copolymer, with small amounts of other monomers added to make it easier to dissolve and spin.

The fibers are formed by dissolving the polymer into a dope and extruding it through fine spinnerets into a coagulating bath, where the filaments solidify. For textile use, this yields the soft acrylic fibers sold under names like Orlon and Creslan. For the far more demanding world of carbon fiber, the PAN fiber is only the beginning — a precursor destined for the furnace.

### Applications and Impact: From Sweaters to Spacecraft

In its most familiar form, PAN is the acrylic in your winter wardrobe — the warm, wool-like fiber in sweaters, blankets, and outdoor fabrics, prized for its softness, its resistance to sunlight, and its ability to hold dye. For decades this was PAN's public face: a humble, affordable textile fiber. It also serves quietly in filtration membranes and hollow fibers for water purification.

But PAN's true significance lies in the furnace. When PAN fiber is slowly oxidized in air and then carbonized at temperatures above a thousand degrees in an inert atmosphere, its carbon atoms rearrange into the strong, light lattices of carbon fiber. Roughly ninety percent of the world's carbon fiber begins life as PAN. That means the skeletons of modern airliners, the bodies of Formula 1 cars, the casings of solid rocket motors, and the frames of high-end bicycles all trace their lineage back to this once-rejected powder. A material deemed useless in 1930 now carries astronauts toward space.

### The Precursor to the Future

As the world races to build lighter aircraft, more efficient vehicles, and larger wind turbines, demand for carbon fiber — and therefore for PAN — continues to climb. Researchers are working to make the energy-intensive conversion from fiber to carbon cheaper and cleaner, and to develop PAN precursors that yield ever stronger, ever lighter carbon fibers. The stubborn powder of 1930 has become one of the most strategically important polymers of the twenty-first century.

The story of polyacrylonitrile is the ultimate tale of delayed recognition. Dismissed as unworkable by the very chemists who made it, it waited patiently for the world to develop the tools and the vision to see its worth. Today, wherever strength must be married to lightness, PAN is there — no longer a footnote, but the very backbone of flight.
